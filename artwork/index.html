Research Statement

Statement of Purpose — AI-Assisted Simulation and Design for Space Hardware

Research Directions

Hover over the diagram

My research sits at the intersection of Physics-Consistent AI, Uncertainty-Aware Optimization, and Deployable Structures —developing computational tools that accelerate engineering iteration while preserving the reliability required for space hardware.

Motivation

The next era of space systems will be shaped less by a single breakthrough than by the ability to design reliable hardware under extreme conditions—thermal cycling, vibration, shock, radiation, and limited chances for repair. At the same time, artificial intelligence is changing how engineers model, optimize, and validate complex physical systems. My research objective is to develop AI-assisted simulation and design methods that speed up engineering iteration while keeping the reliability required for space hardware.

The core problem I want to address is the gap between speed and trust in engineering computation. High-fidelity simulation—such as computational fluid dynamics (CFD) and finite element analysis (FEA)—can be physically accurate, but it becomes slow and expensive when design spaces are large and uncertainty must be considered. Purely data-driven models can be fast, but they may fail when conditions change or when they are asked to predict outside what they have seen before. In space engineering, this is not a minor weakness. The research challenge, therefore, is not simply to "use AI in engineering," but to build AI methods that respect physical laws, represent uncertainty honestly, and can be checked and trusted.

Direction I
Physics-Consistent
Surrogate Models

I plan to develop physics-consistent learning models that serve as reliable surrogates for parts of the simulation pipeline. Rather than replacing physics-based solvers, the goal is to reduce repeated computation in design loops while preserving physical meaning by enforcing governing equations and boundary conditions. I will focus on stability and scalability for stiff systems and complex geometries, using approaches such as domain decomposition, adaptive training, and model designs that behave more like numerical tools than black boxes.

Building on my physics foundation and my ongoing quantum machine-learning-assisted topology optimization work with Tohoku University, I also want to integrate quantum machine learning and quantum algorithms into hybrid design workflows—using them to expand exploration and to tackle combinatorial tasks—while keeping final decisions anchored in trusted physics simulation.

Direction II
Robustness &
Uncertainty-Aware Design

Space systems must work despite manufacturing variation, material uncertainty, and changing environments. This means the best design is often not the one that is optimal on paper, but the one that remains safe and effective under realistic perturbations. I plan to integrate uncertainty quantification and reliability metrics into design optimization, so the workflow produces designs with margins that engineers can justify. This direction combines efficient surrogate modeling, reliability-aware optimization, and verification strategies that determine when high-fidelity simulation must be brought back in. The goal is responsible decision-making, not just fast decision-making.

Direction III
Deployable &
Impact-Tolerant Structures

I want to apply these methods to structural concepts relevant to space hardware: lightweight, deployable, and impact-tolerant structures, including deployable structures for small satellites. My exposure to architected structures and metamaterial-inspired design has shown me that geometry can unlock performance regimes that traditional materials alone cannot. I am actively developing reusable kirigami-based impact absorber concepts, where geometric mechanisms and stability transitions absorb energy without relying on one-time damage. By combining these structural ideas with AI-assisted simulation and reliability-aware optimization, I aim to enable designs that are computationally discoverable, manufacturable, and validated through multi-physics analysis rather than intuition alone.

Background

In a rocket startup environment, I ran injector CFD simulations and learned firsthand how sensitive performance predictions can be to modeling choices—and why reliability requires disciplined verification. In parallel, my PINN-based turbulence research focused on improving learning efficiency without losing physical structure, and I confirmed that incorporating multi-scale basis functions could make the model more stable and accurate on unsteady-flow cases. Across both settings, I repeatedly faced the same tension: moving fast enough to iterate, while keeping results trustworthy enough to act on. Together, these experiences sharpened the problem I now want to solve: closing the gap between speed and trust in engineering computation.

Long-term Vision

Ultimately, my objective is to help build computational foundations for the next era of space systems—where AI accelerates engineering without weakening rigor, and where reliability remains the organizing principle even as iteration becomes faster. Methods become truly valuable when adopted at scale—across laboratories, industry partners, and engineering programs that depend on reliable simulation and fast iteration. I intend to bring these proven workflows into Korea's space and advanced manufacturing landscape, where reliability and scale are everyday requirements, and continue improving them through long-term collaboration with international partners so that teams on both sides can share benchmarks, cross-check assumptions, and refine tools together.
